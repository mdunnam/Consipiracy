FREEMASONRY

33 degrees. The lower levels are a charitable brotherhood. The upper levels serve an agenda most members never see. Albert Pike's own words in Morals and Dogma describe what the 33rd degree actually venerates — and it is not a benevolent deity.

The Degree System — Concealment by Design

Freemasonry is structured as a progression of degrees — from the basic Blue Lodge (1st through 3rd degree) through the York Rite and Scottish Rite branches, reaching 32 degrees in the Scottish Rite (with the honorary 33rd degree granted by selection). The vast majority of Masons never progress beyond the basic degrees. The structure is explicitly designed so that truth about the order's higher purposes is revealed only as a member advances sufficiently — and most members never advance enough to encounter it. This is not speculation: Albert Pike's Morals and Dogma (1871) states explicitly that the lower degrees are given misleading explanations of the symbols that the higher degrees understand correctly.

Albert Pike's Admission

"The Blue Degrees are but the outer court or portico of the Temple. Part of the symbols are displayed there to the Initiate, but he is intentionally misled by false interpretations. It is not intended that he shall understand them; but it is intended that he shall imagine he understands them." — Albert Pike, Morals and Dogma (1871)

The Founders — Masonic Architecture of Power

Freemasonry's concentration in the American founding generation is extraordinary: approximately 9 of the 56 signers of the Declaration of Independence were Masons; between 13 and 28 of the 39 Constitutional Convention signatories were Masons (documented membership varies by source). George Washington, Benjamin Franklin, Paul Revere, John Hancock, John Paul Jones — all confirmed Masons. The architecture of Washington D.C. — designed by Pierre Charles L'Enfant with Masonic geometric principles — incorporates Masonic symbols including inverted pentagrams, the compass and square, and the alignment of key buildings with astrological reference points at the founding date.

The Count of Masonic World Leaders
