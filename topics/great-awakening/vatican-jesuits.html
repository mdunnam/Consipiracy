The Jesuit Structure

The Society of Jesus was founded by Ignatius of Loyola in 1540 with papal approval. Unlike other religious orders, the Jesuits are organised on military lines — with a Superior General (the "Black Pope," so called because of the black cassock, contrasting with the white of the visible Pope) who has supreme authority over all Jesuits, with an oath of absolute obedience. The Jesuits were designed for a specific mission: the educational, intellectual, and diplomatic counter-attack against the Protestant Reformation. They built networks of schools, universities, and missions across every continent, producing generations of graduates loyal to Jesuit-shaped thinking.

The Jesuits have been expelled from Portugal (1759), France (1764), both Sicilies, Malta, Parma (1768), Spain (1767), and suppressed by Pope Clement XIV himself in 1773 — only to be reconstituted in 1814 by Pope Pius VII under Napoleonic pressure. A religious order so dangerous that a pope banned it — and another pope restored it under external political coercion — is not a typical charitable organisation.
