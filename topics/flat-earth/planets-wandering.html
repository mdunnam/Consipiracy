PLANETS: WANDERING LIGHTS

The word "planet" comes from the Greek planetes — wanderer. The ancients saw them as wandering lights on or near the dome. Modern astronomy made them into worlds like Earth — without ever going there to look.

What Planets Actually Are

The five classical planets — Mercury, Venus, Mars, Jupiter, Saturn — were named by ancient astronomers as "wandering stars": lights that moved across the fixed star field of the firmament, following recognisable paths but drifting relative to the fixed stars. They were luminous, they had distinct colours, and they moved — but they were described as lights, not as other Earths. The idea that planets are solid spherical worlds populated by terrain, mountains, and potentially life is a post-Copernican extrapolation built from telescope observations interpreted through the assumption that the heliocentric model is correct.

Flat earth researchers propose that the planets are luminous bodies at or near the level of the dome — brighter and more independently mobile than the fixed stars, but not independent spherical worlds at planetary distances. Their brightness, colour, and size variation as seen from Earth are consistent with variable-luminosity bodies at a relatively close distance, not with Newtonian orbital mechanics governing massive objects hundreds of millions of miles away.

Retrograde Motion — The Defining Anomaly

All five classical planets exhibit retrograde motion — periods where they appear to move backward against the star field before resuming forward motion. In Ptolemaic (pre-Copernican) geocentric astronomy, this was explained by epicycles — small circles the planets moved along while orbiting Earth. Copernicus replaced this with the heliocentric model, claiming retrograde is an optical illusion caused by Earth "overtaking" the outer planets in its orbit. This explanation is mathematically functional but requires accepting the heliocentric model as the frame of reference.

Flat earth researchers point out that the epicycle explanation — with planets executing independent sub-circuits within their main circuits around the flat Earth centre — also fully explains retrograde motion, and was abandoned not because it was disproven but because the heliocentric model made it unnecessary. Retrograde is consistent with any model that has independent circuit-speed variations among the luminaries — it does not prove heliocentrism.

Telescopes Through Atmosphere

Every image of planetary "surfaces" is taken through hundreds of miles of Earth's turbulent atmosphere plus the glass of a telescope. Saturn's "rings," Jupiter's "cloud bands," and Mars's "surface features" are telescope-limited optical interpretations of a luminous disc seen through atmospheric diffraction. Space probes sent to planets are NASA operations — and NASA cannot be verified as an independent source. The flat earth position is not that planets have no features, but that we cannot know from Earth-based observation what their nature truly is.

Planets Do Not Twinkle — What This Means

Unlike stars, the five classical planets appear as stable, non-twinkling lights to the naked eye. Mainstream explanation: planets are close enough to show as tiny discs rather than point sources, so atmospheric turbulence averages out across the disc rather than producing the scintillation of a point source. Flat earth alternative: planets are independent luminaries of sufficient size and proximity that their light is stable — consistent with being local lights above the Earth's atmosphere at a closer range than the fixed star dome, where atmospheric effects are less disruptive.

Venus as a Local Luminary

Venus — called the "morning star" and "evening star" — has a predictable, cyclical path that follows the sun closely and never moves far from the sun's circuit. It appears at specific points of the sun's circuit with remarkable regularity. On a flat earth, Venus follows a tight circuit around the same dome region as the sun. On the heliocentric model, its appearance is explained by orbital periods and Earth's overtaking — both models make identical predictions.

The "Are They Inhabited?" Question

Every major world religion and philosophical tradition described the planets as divine lights or spiritual presences — not inhabited worlds. The idea of populated planetary worlds emerged with the heliocentric revolution and was specifically used to challenge the unique significance of Earth and humanity in the cosmological order. Whether deliberate or not, the effect was to reframe humanity as one of billions of possible inhabitants of a random universe — dismantling the cosmological centrality of the flat Earth model's human-scale world.
