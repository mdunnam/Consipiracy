Operation Mockingbird

Operation Mockingbird was a CIA programme, begun in the late 1940s under Frank Wisner, to recruit American journalists, editors, and media executives to serve as intelligence assets. Its existence was confirmed by the Church Committee of the U.S. Senate Select Committee on Intelligence in 1975, which found that by 1953 the CIA had relationships with over 400 American journalists, including employees of some of the most prestigious news organisations in the country.

These assets were not simply tasked with planting stories favourable to CIA operations. They were used to suppress coverage of stories the agency wanted kept out of public discourse, to discredit journalists pursuing inconvenient investigations, and to build the linguistic and conceptual framework through which the public understood international affairs — ensuring that CIA-preferred interpretations of events became the default reality accepted by Western publics.

CIA Director William Colby told a congressional committee in 1977: "The Central Intelligence Agency owns everyone of any significance in the major media." Former CIA Director William Casey reportedly said: "We'll know our disinformation program is complete when everything the American public believes is false."

Church Committee Finding

The Senate Select Committee to Study Governmental Operations with Respect to Intelligence Activities (the Church Committee), in its 1975 final report, confirmed the CIA was operating a media manipulation programme involving hundreds of domestic journalists and media executives. No prosecutions followed. The programme was never formally dissolved.

Media Consolidation Timeline

The consolidation of media ownership from 50 companies to 6 in four decades did not occur through normal market competition. It was facilitated by systematic regulatory changes: the loosening of cross-ownership rules, the raising of audience reach caps, and the elimination of the FCC Fairness Doctrine in 1987 — which had required broadcasters to present controversial public issues in a balanced manner.

Each of these regulatory changes was advocated for by the same revolving-door lobby apparatus that connected broadcast company boards to the FCC itself. The result was not a free market in media — it was a managed consolidation process that produced the tight oligopoly currently in place. Six companies is not a competitive market. It is a cartel.

Hollywood: Social Programming

The Pentagon's Entertainment Liaison Office has operated since World War II providing military hardware, personnel, and access to film productions in exchange for script review and modification rights. Productions that refuse Pentagon changes lose access to the resources that make their productions viable.

Sociologist Matthew Alford and political scientist Robbie Graham have documented hundreds of films where DoD script requests changed plot lines, character motivations, and ending outcomes to present the military in a more favourable light, exclude depictions of genuine war crimes, and embed specific recruitment messaging.

  • History rewriting: The systematic alteration or omission of historical facts about CIA operations, banking crises, political assassinations, and state crimes in "based on a true story" popular entertainment that shapes mass historical understanding.

Social Media & Algorithmic Censorship

The promise of the early internet — a genuinely decentralised, democratic information commons — was neutralised through the consolidation of internet traffic through a small number of platform monopolies (Google, YouTube, Facebook/Meta, Twitter/X) and the implementation of algorithm-driven content modulation that shapes what users see far more powerfully than any government broadcast regulator.

The Twitter Files — internal documents released by Elon Musk in 2022 — provided documented evidence that Twitter's content moderation was conducted in direct coordination with the FBI, the DHS, the CIA, and other federal agencies. Specific accounts, hashtags, and stories were suppressed on government request. "Shadow banning" — reducing the reach of accounts without informing users — was actively used against political content the government wished to suppress.

Facebook's collaboration with the CIA was documented in its own board composition and funding: In-Q-Tel (the CIA's venture capital arm) was a documented early Facebook investor. Mark Zuckerberg's early collaboration with DARPA's LifeLog project — a database of everything a person sees, hears, and does — which was cancelled on the same day Facebook was incorporated, has been noted by numerous researchers as a connection that merits scrutiny.

The "Fact-Checker" Racket

"Fact-checking" organisations — PolitiFact, Snopes, NewsGuard, the Associated Press Fact Check,, and affiliated services — are presented as independent truth arbiters protecting public discourse from misinformation. They are, in practice, gatekeeping operations funded by the same foundations and corporate entities that benefit from the narratives they protect.

NewsGuard, the most influential media trust-scoring service used by social media platforms and search engines to rank "trustworthy" outlets, is funded by GlaxoSmithKline, Publicis Groupe (which represents pharmaceutical companies including Pfizer and Sanofi), and has received funding connected to the U.S. State Department. Its board includes operatives from major intelligence and foreign policy institutions. "Trustworthy" outlets reliably receive high scores. Outlets that contradict pharmaceutical or government narratives reliably receive low scores.

The function of the fact-checker is not to determine truth. It is to provide an institutional veneer of legitimacy to the suppression of inconvenient information — to allow platforms to demonetise, deboost, and deplatform content while claiming to be merely following "independent verification standards."

Becoming Your Own Media

The antidote to a captured media system is a literate, independent research culture. Every person who learns to verify primary sources, to follow financial relationships, to check institutional affiliations, and to cross-reference claims across genuinely independent outlets is a node in an alternative information network that the control system cannot easily replicate or suppress.

  • Primary sources first. Always read the actual document, study, or legislation — not the mainstream media's summary of it. The divergence between the two is frequently instructive.
  • Follow the funding. Before trusting any study, report, or organisation, identify who funds it. If a pharmaceutical company funds a study about its own product, the result is not independent science.
  • Institutional affiliations matter. When a talking head appears on cable news to provide "expert analysis," look up their institutional affiliations. Most serve on boards or advisory committees of the industries they comment on.
  • The excluded matters more than the included. What mainstream media consistently does not cover tells you as much as what it does. The sustained non-coverage of central banking, the Federal Reserve's private ownership structure, and the BIS is not accidental.
